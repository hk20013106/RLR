# Darwin (L9) - Biological Interpretation

## Convergent evolution context
Sk (Scotophilus kuhlii, bat, Chiroptera) and Sm (Suncus murinus, shrew, Eulipotyphla) independently evolved high heart rates (>500 bpm in shrews, >400 in small bats). Rn (Rattus norvegicus) has ~300-400 bpm as baseline.
Convergent molecular signatures would indicate that different lineages arrived at similar cardiac gene expression programs under similar selective pressure.

## What convergence might look like
1. Metabolic: upregulated OXPHOS, fatty acid oxidation, mitochondrial biogenesis (high ATP demand)
2. Calcium cycling: SERCA, RyR, NCX upregulated (faster relaxation-contraction cycle)
3. Structural: sarcomere organization, ECM remodeling
4. Electrophysiology: ion channel remodeling

## Current evidence interpretation
The all-sample network shows species-specific modules, NOT convergence.
This is biologically plausible: even if Sk and Sm both have high heart rates, the underlying gene expression changes may be lineage-specific (different mutations, different pathways).
True convergence at the co-expression level may only appear in specific chambers or specific functional gene sets.

## Literature expectation
Convergent transcriptomic signatures have been reported in echolocating mammals (bats + dolphins) for hearing genes. For cardiac adaptation, convergence is less established.
A null result (species-specific modules, no convergence) is scientifically valid and publishable.

## Risk of overinterpretation
Do not claim convergent adaptation from weak module overlap. Separation of correlation, mechanism, and adaptation is essential. Module preservation statistics are statistical, not mechanistic proof.
